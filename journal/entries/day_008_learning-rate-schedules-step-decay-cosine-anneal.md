# Day 8 — Learning rate schedules: step decay, cosine annealing, warm restarts

> 📅 June 25, 2026

---

## 🧠 Concept Explanation

Learning rate schedules dynamically adjust the step size during training, allowing large steps early for rapid exploration and smaller steps later for fine‑grained convergence. Step decay reduces the rate by a fixed factor every N epochs (e.g., halve every 30 epochs), which is simple but can cause abrupt changes. Cosine annealing follows a smooth cosine curve from an initial maximum to a minimum over a predefined period, providing a gradual decay that often yields better final performance. Warm restarts (SGDR) extend cosine annealing by periodically resetting the learning rate to its maximum, letting the optimizer escape shallow minima and explore new regions; each restart can use a longer period (T_mult > 1) to gradually increase exploration time. All three strategies share the goal of balancing exploration and exploitation without manual tuning at every epoch. In practice, cosine annealing with warm restarts often outperforms plain step decay on deep vision and language models because the periodic reheating prevents premature convergence while the smooth decay avoids the instability of sudden drops. Choosing a schedule depends on dataset size, model capacity, and compute budget; a common heuristic is to start with cosine annealing and add restarts if validation loss plateaus.

---

## ✅ Key Points

- Step decay: multiply LR by a constant factor at fixed epoch intervals.
- Cosine annealing: LR follows a cosine curve from max to min over a cycle.
- Warm restarts (SGDR): periodically reset LR to max, optionally lengthening cycles.
- Smooth schedules reduce oscillations and improve final generalization.
- Schedule choice interacts with batch size, optimizer, and total epochs.

```
Cosine Annealing Formula:
lr_t = lr_min + 0.5 * (lr_max - lr_min) * (1 + cos(pi * t / T_max))
```

## 💡 Real-World Analogy

Like a hiker descending a mountain: start with big strides (high LR), gradually shorten steps (cosine decay), and occasionally climb back up to a ridge (warm restart) to find a better path down.

---

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes

- Using a fixed LR for all epochs, ignoring the benefit of decay.
- Setting the decay period too short, causing the LR to vanish before convergence.
- Applying warm restarts without increasing the cycle length, leading to repeated instability.
