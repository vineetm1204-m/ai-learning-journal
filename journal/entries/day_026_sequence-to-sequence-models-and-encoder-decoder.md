# Day 26 — Sequence-to-sequence models and encoder-decoder

## 🧠 Concept Explanation

Sequence-to-sequence (seq2seq) models map an input sequence of arbitrary length to an output sequence of arbitrary length, making them ideal for tasks like machine translation, summarization, and speech recognition. The classic architecture consists of two recurrent neural networks: an encoder that reads the source tokens one by one and compresses the entire sequence into a fixed‑size context vector (the final hidden state), and a decoder that generates the target tokens step by step, conditioned on that context vector and its own previous predictions. During training, teacher forcing feeds the ground‑truth previous token to the decoder, while at inference the decoder uses its own predictions. Attention mechanisms later augment this design by allowing the decoder to look back at all encoder hidden states, alleviating the bottleneck of a single context vector and improving alignment. The encoder and decoder can be built from LSTMs, GRUs, or Transformers; the latter replaces recurrence with self‑attention, enabling parallel training and better long‑range dependencies. Seq2seq models are trained end‑to‑end with cross‑entropy loss over the target vocabulary, and techniques such as beam search, length normalization, and coverage penalties are used at decoding time to produce fluent, accurate outputs.

---

## ✅ Key Points

- Encoder compresses variable‑length input into a context representation.
- Decoder generates output token by token, conditioned on context and previous tokens.
- Teacher forcing accelerates training by feeding ground‑truth history.
- Attention lets the decoder attend to all encoder states, solving the bottleneck.
- Transformers replace recurrence with self‑attention for parallelism and longer context.

## 💡 Real-World Analogy

Think of a translator who first reads an entire foreign‑language paragraph, memorizes its meaning in a mental summary, and then speaks the translation sentence by sentence, occasionally glancing back at the original text for nuance.

---

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes

- Using a single fixed‑size context vector for very long sequences without attention, causing information loss.
- Forgetting to mask padding tokens in the loss, so the model learns to predict padding.
- Applying teacher forcing at inference time, which leads to exposure bias and error accumulation.
