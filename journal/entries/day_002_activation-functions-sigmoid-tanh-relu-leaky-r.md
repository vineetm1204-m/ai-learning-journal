## 🧠 Concept Explanation

Activation functions introduce non‑linearity into neural networks, allowing them to model complex patterns beyond simple linear combinations. The sigmoid squashes inputs to (0,1), making it useful for probabilities but it saturates for large magnitudes, causing vanishing gradients. Tanh is similar but zero‑centered, outputting (‑1,1), which often speeds up convergence. ReLU (max(0,x)) is piecewise linear, cheap to compute, and avoids saturation for positive values, yet it can produce dead neurons that never activate. Leaky ReLU adds a small slope for negative inputs (αx, α≈0.01), mitigating the dying‑ReLU problem while keeping computational simplicity. GELU (x·Φ(x)) multiplies the input by the Gaussian CDF, providing a smooth, probabilistic gating that works well in transformer models. Choosing an activation depends on depth, normalization, and task: sigmoid/tanh for shallow nets or output layers, ReLU/Leaky ReLU for deep CNNs, and GELU for attention‑based architectures. The derivative of sigmoid is σ(x)(1‑σ(x)), which peaks at 0.25 and shrinks near the tails, limiting gradient magnitude. Tanh’s derivative 1‑tanh²(x) has a maximum of 1, giving stronger gradients around zero. ReLU’s derivative is 1 for x>0 and 0 otherwise, so gradients flow unchanged through active units but vanish for inactive ones. Leaky ReLU’s derivative is 1 for x>0 and α for x<0, preserving a small gradient everywhere. GELU’s derivative involves the Gaussian PDF, yielding a smooth transition that adapts to the input distribution. In practice, batch normalization combined with ReLU or GELU often yields the fastest training, while sigmoid and tanh remain common in recurrent gating mechanisms.

## ✅ Key Points

- Sigmoid maps to (0,1) but suffers vanishing gradients for large |x|.
- Tanh is zero‑centered with range (‑1,1), giving stronger gradients near zero.
- ReLU is cheap and non‑saturating for x>0, but can create dead neurons.
- Leaky ReLU adds a tiny negative slope (α≈0.01) to keep gradients alive.
- GELU uses the Gaussian CDF for smooth probabilistic gating, popular in transformers.

## 🏗️ Diagram / Formula

```
sigmoid(x) = 1 / (1 + exp(-x))
tanh(x)   = (exp(x) - exp(-x)) / (exp(x) + exp(-x))
ReLU(x)   = max(0, x)
LeakyReLU = x if x>0 else α*x   (α≈0.01)
GELU(x)   = x * Φ(x)  where Φ is Gaussian CDF
```

## 💡 Real-World Analogy

Activation functions are like dimmer switches: sigmoid and tanh are smooth knobs that saturate at the ends, ReLU is a simple on/off switch that stays on once pushed, Leaky ReLU lets a tiny current leak even when off, and GELU is a smart dimmer that adapts its brightness based on the room’s lighting statistics.

---

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes

- Using sigmoid or tanh in deep networks, leading to vanishing gradients.
- Leaving Leaky ReLU’s α at zero, which reverts to plain ReLU and dead neurons.
- Applying ReLU on layers that must output negative values (e.g., regression targets).
