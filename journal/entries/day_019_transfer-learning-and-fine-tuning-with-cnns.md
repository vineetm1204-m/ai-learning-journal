# Day 19 — Transfer learning and fine-tuning with CNNs

## 🧠 Concept Explanation

Transfer learning leverages a model pretrained on a large dataset such as ImageNet and adapts it to a new, often smaller, task. The pretrained convolutional base has already learned generic visual features like edges, textures, and object parts. Instead of training a network from scratch, we keep these learned filters and only modify the final classification layers to match the target classes. Fine‑tuning goes a step further by unfreezing some of the higher‑level layers and continuing training with a low learning rate, allowing the model to specialize while preserving the useful low‑level representations. This approach dramatically reduces data requirements, training time, and risk of overfitting. In practice, one typically replaces the original fully‑connected head with a new head, freezes the backbone, trains the head for a few epochs, then optionally unfreezes the last few blocks and trains the whole network with a smaller learning rate. Because the early layers capture universal patterns, they transfer well across domains such as medical imaging, satellite photos, or artistic style classification. Monitoring validation loss during fine‑tuning prevents catastrophic forgetting, where the model overwrites useful features.

---

## ✅ Key Points

- Pretrained CNNs provide generic feature extractors that work across many vision tasks.
- Freeze the backbone initially; train only the new task‑specific head.
- Fine‑tune by unfreezing higher layers with a very low learning rate.
- Use smaller learning rates and early stopping to avoid catastrophic forgetting.
- Transfer learning drastically cuts data and compute needs compared to training from scratch.

## 💡 Real-World Analogy

Like a master chef teaching a novice a signature sauce: the novice keeps the chef’s base recipe (pretrained features) and only tweaks the final seasoning (new head) for a different dish.

---

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes

- Unfreezing the entire network too early with a high learning rate, destroying pretrained features.
- Using the original classifier head without adapting it to the new number of classes.
- Neglecting to adjust data preprocessing (e.g., normalization) to match the pretrained model’s expectations.
