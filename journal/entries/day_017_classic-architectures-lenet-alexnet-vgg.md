## 🧠 Concept Explanation

LeNet (1998), AlexNet (2012), and VGG (2014) form the foundational lineage of modern convolutional neural networks. LeNet-5, designed by Yann LeCun for handwritten digit recognition, introduced the core paradigm: alternating convolution and pooling layers followed by fully connected layers. It used small 5x5 kernels, sigmoid/tanh activations, and average pooling, proving that gradient-based learning could work on raw pixel data. AlexNet, created by Krizhevsky, Sutskever, and Hinton, scaled this idea dramatically. It employed ReLU activations for faster training, dropout for regularization, data augmentation, and overlapped max pooling. Crucially, it leveraged two GPUs to train a deep, 60-million-parameter model on ImageNet, winning the 2012 competition by a large margin and igniting the deep learning boom. VGG (Visual Geometry Group) from Oxford further simplified architecture design by using only 3x3 convolutional filters stacked deeper (up to 19 layers). This uniform structure showed that depth itself is a critical factor for performance, and the 3x3 kernel became a standard building block. Together, these architectures established the blueprint: deep stacks of small convolutions, non-linearities, pooling, and fully connected heads, trained end-to-end with backpropagation on large datasets.

---

## ✅ Key Points

- LeNet pioneered the Conv-Pool-FC pattern for digit recognition.
- AlexNet popularized ReLU, dropout, and GPU training at scale.
- VGG demonstrated that stacking many 3x3 layers outperforms larger kernels.
- Depth and small filters became the dominant design principle.
- These models enabled transfer learning and modern CNN variants.

## 💡 Real-World Analogy

If LeNet is a simple point-and-shoot camera, AlexNet is a professional DSLR with interchangeable lenses, and VGG is a modular camera system where you stack many high-quality prime lenses to capture finer details.

---

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes

- Assuming larger kernels (e.g., 7x7) always capture more context than stacked 3x3 layers.
- Ignoring the importance of ReLU and dropout introduced by AlexNet for training deep nets.
- Using the original fully connected layer sizes (e.g., 4096) on small datasets causing severe overfitting.
