# Day 1 — Perceptrons and the biological neuron analogy

> 📅 June 15, 2026

---

## 🧠 Concept Explanation

A perceptron is the simplest artificial neuron, inspired by how a biological neuron receives signals through dendrites, integrates them in the cell body, and fires an output through the axon if the combined input exceeds a threshold. In the model, each input x_i is multiplied by a learnable weight w_i, the products are summed together with a bias term b, and the result passes through an activation function (originally a step function) to produce a binary output. This linear‑threshold unit can implement logical gates such as AND, OR, and NAND, showing that a single neuron can perform basic computation. However, a lone perceptron cannot solve non‑linearly separable problems like XOR; stacking multiple perceptrons into layers creates a multilayer network that can approximate any continuous function. Training adjusts the weights using the perceptron learning rule: when the prediction is wrong, the weights are nudged in the direction of the correct label. This iterative error‑correction mirrors synaptic plasticity in the brain, where connections strengthen or weaken based on activity. Understanding the perceptron lays the groundwork for modern deep learning, where millions of such units are composed into deep architectures.

---

## ✅ Key Points

- Inputs are weighted, summed, and passed through a threshold activation.
- The bias term shifts the decision boundary without needing an extra input.
- A single perceptron solves only linearly separable tasks.
- The perceptron learning rule updates weights only on misclassified examples.
- Stacking perceptrons yields multilayer networks capable of universal approximation.

## 💡 Real-World Analogy

Think of a perceptron as a tiny voting committee: each member (input) casts a weighted vote, the chair (bias) adds a baseline preference, and the final decision (output) is made only if the total votes cross a fixed threshold.

---

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes

- Assuming a single perceptron can learn XOR or any non‑linear pattern.
- Forgetting to include the bias term, which limits the decision boundary to pass through the origin.
- Using a continuous activation (e.g., sigmoid) while still applying the original perceptron update rule, which expects a step function.
