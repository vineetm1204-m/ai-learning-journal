## 🧠 Concept Explanation

Optimizers guide how a neural network updates its weights to minimize a loss function. Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) takes a step proportional to the negative gradient of a mini‑batch, but its fixed learning rate can cause slow convergence or oscillations. Momentum adds a velocity term that accumulates past gradients, allowing the optimizer to accelerate in consistent directions and dampen oscillations, effectively smoothing the path. RMSProp adapts the learning rate per parameter by dividing the gradient by a running average of its recent magnitudes, which helps with non‑stationary objectives and varying gradient scales. Adam combines Momentum and RMSProp: it maintains both a first‑moment estimate (mean of gradients) and a second‑moment estimate (uncentered variance), then corrects their bias early in training. AdamW improves Adam by decoupling weight decay from the adaptive learning rate, applying regularization directly to the weights rather than to the gradient‑scaled update, which yields better generalization especially for transformer models. Choosing an optimizer depends on the architecture, dataset size, and training stability requirements; in practice AdamW with a cosine learning‑rate schedule is a strong default for modern deep learning.

---

## ✅ Key Points

- SGD updates weights using a fixed learning rate times the mini‑batch gradient.
- Momentum accumulates a velocity vector to accelerate consistent gradient directions.
- RMSProp normalizes gradients by a moving average of their squared magnitudes.
- Adam merges Momentum and RMSProp with bias‑corrected first and second moments.
- AdamW separates weight decay from the adaptive step, improving regularization.

## 🏗️ Diagram / Formula

```
SGD:       w <- w - lr * g
Momentum:  v <- beta * v + (1-beta) * g
           w <- w - lr * v
RMSProp:   s <- gamma * s + (1-gamma) * g^2
           w <- w - lr * g / (sqrt(s) + eps)
Adam:      m <- beta1 * m + (1-beta1) * g
           v <- beta2 * v + (1-beta2) * g^2
           m_hat = m / (1-beta1^t), v_hat = v / (1-beta2^t)
           w <- w - lr * m_hat / (sqrt(v_hat) + eps)
AdamW:     w <- w - lr * (m_hat / (sqrt(v_hat) + eps) + weight_decay * w)
```

## 💡 Real-World Analogy

Think of SGD as a hiker taking fixed‑size steps downhill; Momentum gives the hiker a heavy backpack that keeps rolling forward, RMSProp adjusts step size based on terrain steepness, and Adam equips the hiker with both a momentum backpack and adaptive boots, while AdamW adds a gentle tug toward the trailhead to prevent wandering.

---

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes

- Using a too‑large learning rate with Adam/AdamW, causing divergence despite adaptive scaling.
- Applying weight decay inside the Adam update (as in original Adam) instead of the decoupled AdamW form.
- Neglecting learning‑rate schedules; constant rates often lead to sub‑optimal convergence.
